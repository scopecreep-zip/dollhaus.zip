Our Government

Understanding the structure and principles of our democratic system

The Constitution of the United Dolls establishes a federal democratic republic form of government that divides power among three separate branches to ensure no single entity holds too much authority.

The Three Branches of Government

Legislative Branch

The Congress - Composed of the House of Representatives and the Senate

Primary Responsibilities:

  • Makes all federal laws
  • Declares war
  • Regulates interstate and foreign commerce
  • Controls taxing and spending policies
  • Confirms or rejects presidential appointments

Executive Branch

The President - Serves as head of state and commander-in-chief

Primary Responsibilities:

  • Enforces and implements federal laws
  • Conducts foreign policy and diplomacy
  • Commands the armed forces
  • Appoints federal judges and cabinet members
  • Signs or vetoes legislation

Judicial Branch

The Supreme Court and Federal Courts - Interprets the law

Primary Responsibilities:

  • Interprets the meaning of laws
  • Applies laws to individual cases
  • Decides if laws violate the Constitution
  • Protects individual rights
  • Resolves disputes between states

Checks and Balances

The Founding Dolls designed a system where each branch of government has the ability to limit the powers of the other branches, preventing any one branch from becoming too powerful.

Examples of Checks and Balances:

  • Legislative checks on Executive: Can override presidential vetoes, reject appointments, impeach the President
  • Executive checks on Legislative: Can veto legislation, call special sessions of Congress
  • Judicial checks on Legislative: Can declare laws unconstitutional
  • Judicial checks on Executive: Can declare executive actions unconstitutional
  • Legislative checks on Judicial: Can impeach judges, approve judicial appointments
  • Executive checks on Judicial: Appoints federal judges

The Great Compromise

During the Constitutional Convention, delegates faced a critical challenge: how to fairly represent both large and small states in the new legislature. The Great Compromise (also known as the Connecticut Compromise) created a bicameral legislature:

House of Representatives

Representation based on population

Larger states have more representatives, ensuring proportional representation.

Senate

Equal representation for all states

Each state has two senators, ensuring equal voice for all states.

The Bill of Rights

The first ten amendments to the Constitution, ratified in 1791, guarantee fundamental rights and freedoms to all dolls:

First Amendment: Freedom of religion, speech, press, assembly, and petition
Second Amendment: Right to bear arms
Third Amendment: Protection from quartering of soldiers
Fourth Amendment: Protection from unreasonable search and seizure
Fifth Amendment: Rights in criminal cases, including due process and protection against self-incrimination
Sixth Amendment: Right to a fair and speedy trial
Seventh Amendment: Right to trial by jury in civil cases
Eighth Amendment: Protection from cruel and unusual punishment
Ninth Amendment: Rights retained by the people
Tenth Amendment: Powers reserved to the states
